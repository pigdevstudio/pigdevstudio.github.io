Hi!

I’m Henrique Campos, a game designer with a passion for systems design. Ever since I understood that games are systems of incentives and everything, from the visual effects to their economies, should be designed to guide players toward a desired experience, I got obsessed with understanding the human mind and how it reacts to incentives.

Games and Prototypes

I use games to test and try the knowledge I’m gathering through my journey. They are my lab, where I test if my concepts and hypotheses will work in reality.

Moon Cheeser

A game about a mouse that discovered that the moon is made out of cheese.

On Moon Cheeser, players collect cheese crumbs to spend on skins and unlock new levels.

The game’s core loop is:

  1. Throw comets on the moon to create cheese crumbs
  2. Collect the cheese crumbs to unlock new skins and features
  3. Use the new features to improve the ability to gather more cheese
  4. Repeat

Use the button below to play a demo version of the game available on itch.io.

Moon Cheeser was my first project and I used it mainly to test out my ability to create interesting but simple core loops. This version in particular is not the most up to date one. The most recent version was available on Play Store. After Android updates, I decided to drop support for the game.

Fermi Parallax

In ufology, there’s a theory known as Fermi Paradox where scientist Enrico Fermi pointed out the discrepancy between the lack of conclusive evidence of advanced extraterrestrial life and the apparent likelihood of its existence. Based on that, I created a vertical space shooting game where players transverse some examples of what Fermi called “Big Filters”, which are possible causes that may prevent advanced civilizations from interacting with each other.

The game’s core loop is:

  1. Defeat the current wave of enemies
  2. Unlock new parts of the narrative
  3. Overcome a Big Filter

Use the button below to play a demo version of the game available on itch.io.

In Fermi Parallax, a wordplay with the parallax effect yet another inspiration, I wanted to test my ability to turn a concept into a game. My main inspiration for the game was the famous science YouTube channel Kurzgesagt’s The Fermi Paradox - Where Are All The Aliens? video.

Sunset

One of my core beliefs as a game designer is that I express myself through games. I never understood why people imbued a negative connotation to games, as in “are you playing games with me” or “is this a game to you”. I see politics, the economy, cooking, shopping, and professional and personal relationships through the lens of games, as systems of incentives toward a goal.

In 2021 I met the only person who understood that and wanted to play with me. She was amazing. We were at a park and she said

Let’s play a game? Whoever guess the time when the sun will set, wins!

We spent the sunset drinking and laughing. I won, by the way. But I also wanted to use this concept and turn it into a real, digital game.

Sunset is a game inspired by this memory, which I keep deep in my heart. The core loop is:

  1. Get a sunset guessing time
  2. Manipulate the sun by making it hop or fall to match the guessed time
  3. Score based on your actions

The game is available on itch.io and is preferably played on mobile through the link below:

With Sunset I also wanted to test my ability to keep someone engaged with the simpler mechanic I could think of. In the game, you tap the screen to make the sun hop a little, preventing it from setting before its time. And you can hold the finger on the screen to make it accelerate downwards.

That’s it, but the addicting part is that each of these actions allows you to score differently. Each hop gives you a default amount of points but once you hit the ceiling, you can’t hop. By holding the finger on the screen, you score a dynamic amount of points based on the traveled distance, but you run the risk of performing a premature sunset if the sun hits the horizon. The main challenge is to score the most amount of points while keeping a balance between these two mechanisms.

Talking about mechanisms, something I love to do is create Machinations diagrams.

Machinations

Machinations is a diagram language created by Earnest Adams to model games’ internal economies. In that sense, internal economies are the flow of abstract and concrete resources in a game. Abstract resources can be anything that is not tangible, like threat, competitive advantage, and value. Concrete resources are anything we can count discreetly such as gold coins, lumber, pieces on a board, and fruits on a Pac-man level.

I met Machinations through the book Game Mechanics: Advanced Game Design where Earnest Adams and Joris Dormans collaborated to create what, to me, is a masterpiece.

This modeling language transcended the scope of games and nowadays is used to model any type of system, from finances to energy. I was able to create a model for human brain’s rewarding system based on Jakk Panksepp’s Neuropsycanalitical approach. You can play with the diagram below.
